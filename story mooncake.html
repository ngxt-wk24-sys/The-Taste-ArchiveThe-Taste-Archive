Mooncake

Originated in China
especially during the Tang and Yuan Dynasties.

Mooncakes are traditional Chinese pastries most commonly enjoyed during the Mid-Autumn Festival. Round and richly filled, mooncakes symbolize reunion, completeness, and harmony, reflecting the full moon admired on this special night.

According to legend, mooncakes are closely linked to the story of Chang’e, the Moon Goddess. It is said that Chang’e drank an elixir of immortality and ascended to the moon, leaving her husband behind on Earth. On the night of the full moon, families would offer mooncakes as a tribute, expressing longing, love, and remembrance.

Another historical tale dates back to the Yuan Dynasty, when secret messages were hidden inside mooncakes to coordinate a rebellion. What began as a symbol of resistance later evolved into a symbol of unity and shared hope.

Traditionally, mooncakes are shared rather than eaten alone. Families gather, cut the mooncake into equal pieces, and enjoy it together while admiring the moon. This act represents togetherness and the importance of family bonds.

Today, mooncakes come in many styles—from classic lotus seed paste with salted egg yolk to modern flavors like ice-cream and chocolate. While the fillings may change, the meaning remains the same: a celebration of reunion, tradition, and the warmth of home.
