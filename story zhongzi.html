Food Story

Zongzi

Originated in China
especially during the Warring States Period.

Zongzi are traditional glutinous rice dumplings wrapped in bamboo or reed leaves, most commonly eaten during the Dragon Boat Festival. Their pyramid or cone-like shape and fragrant leaves make them a symbol of remembrance, loyalty, and cultural heritage.

The origin of zongzi is closely connected to the patriotic poet Qu Yuan. According to legend, Qu Yuan drowned himself in the Miluo River after being exiled and witnessing the fall of his country. Villagers, grieving his death, threw rice wrapped in leaves into the river to prevent fish from eating his body. Over time, this act became a tradition, and zongzi were made each year to honor his spirit.

In ancient times, families gathered to soak rice, prepare fillings, and carefully wrap zongzi by hand. The process required patience and skill, passing down techniques and stories from one generation to the next.

Today, zongzi come in many regional varieties—sweet or savory, filled with red beans, dates, pork, or chestnuts. Though flavors differ, zongzi remain a powerful reminder of history, respect, and the enduring bond between tradition and family.
